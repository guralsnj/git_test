A detailed, self-guided screening for ADHD & autistic traits

What this is

This tool walks you through three well-established, publicly available screening questionnaires used in research and clinical settings. It scores your answers, breaks the results down by sub-area, and explains why each result came out the way it did.

93 questions total. Most people finish in 12–18 minutes. You can move at your own pace — progress is kept in your browser only.

Important — this is a screener, not a diagnosis. No questionnaire can diagnose ADHD or autism. Only a qualified clinician can, through a full history, developmental information, and ruling out other explanations (anxiety, depression, trauma, sleep, thyroid, etc.). A high score means "traits worth exploring with a professional," not "you have this condition." A low score does not rule anything out, especially for people who mask heavily or were socialised to hide traits. Use these results as a starting point for a conversation, never as a conclusion.
If any question brings up distressing feelings, it's fine to stop. If you're in crisis, contact a local emergency line or, in the US, call or text 988.

A little context (optional)

Not scored — it only tailors a few notes in your results. Leave blank if you prefer.

Why ask? Autism and ADHD are historically under-diagnosed in women, girls, and people assigned female at birth, partly because they tend to mask more. We use this only to add a note, never to change your score.

What to do next

  • If several scores were elevated: consider talking to your GP/primary-care doctor, a psychologist, or a psychiatrist about an assessment. Bring specific examples from daily life, not just the numbers.
  • Write down real situations — school reports, work feedback, childhood memories, what people close to you notice. Assessors weigh lifelong, cross-situational patterns.
  • Masking matters: a high CAT-Q with a "borderline" AQ can mean traits are real but hidden through effort. Mention this to an assessor.
  • Co-occurrence is common: ADHD and autism frequently overlap (often called AuDHD), and both overlap with anxiety and depression. One elevated score doesn't exclude others.
